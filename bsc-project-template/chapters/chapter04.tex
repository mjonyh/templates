\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

The experimental values obtained for Quartz Glass and Fused Silica in Table \ref{tab:material_properties} align closely with standard literature indexes \cite{boyd2020nonlinear}. However, the highly non-linear SF59 glass exhibited substantial discrepancies between simulation and experiments under high input intensity profiles.

\section{Interpretation of Transmission Profiles}
As observed in Figure \ref{fig:transmission_plot}, the peak-to-valley profile indicates a positive third-order non-linear refractive index ($n_2 > 0$), corresponding to a self-focusing behavior. Had the valley preceded the peak, it would signify a self-defocusing response ($n_2 < 0$), commonly observed in materials like semiconductors under high-intensity pulses.

\section{Analysis of Experimental Uncertainties}
We identified several factors that contributed to experimental fluctuations\footnote{This was checked during system calibration steps under continuous beam laser profiles.}:
\begin{quote}
    ``Fluctuations in laser output power ($1-2\%$), impurities inside glass substrate channels, and minor errors in motorized rail adjustments caused coordinates deviation up to $\pm \SI{0.5}{\milli\meter}$.''
\end{quote}
Such alignment uncertainties must be minimized by adopting automatic photodiode averaging algorithms.
